## 2. What separates a $150 job from a $400+ job

These are the variables Tommy must learn to *see* on a walk-up so he stops under-quoting.
This list feeds the Bucket C quote skeleton directly.

1. **Total square footage** — the base driver. ~$25–$50 per extra 100 sq ft (L2).
2. **One story vs two** — two-story is **+30–50%** (L2): more time, ladders/extension, risk.
3. **Surface material** — vinyl is easiest/cheapest; **brick, stucco, wood, painted surfaces**
   need slower chemistry/lower pressure = more time = more money. Porous (+$0.02/sq ft at Duck).
4. **Severity of growth** — light algae vs heavy black mold/lichen. Heavy neglect = **+20–40%**.
5. **Access difficulty** — tight landscaping, slopes, fences, screened porches, second-story
   peaks, things to bag/tape (cameras, outlets, lights).
6. **Stain type** — oil (+$25–$75), rust (+$25–$50), red-clay deep stain (extra passes), paint
   overspray (maybe not removable).
7. **Prep + protection load** — pre-wetting plants, taping fixtures, moving furniture. The more
   to protect, the more time billed.
8. **Chemical needs** — heavier growth = more SH/surfactant = real material cost.
9. **Bundle vs single surface** — multi-surface days carry the day's revenue; singles are thin.

**The $150 job:** small single-story concrete or a tiny pad, light dirt, easy access, fast.
**The $400+ job:** full house wash on a 2,000+ sf home, some growth, plants to protect, maybe a
driveway tacked on. Same half-day of Tommy's life — **he must price the second one as the
second one, not as "a wash."**

## 3. Technique & safety basics per surface (so Tommy doesn't wreck a customer's property job one)

**The one rule that prevents most disasters: START WIDE, START FAR, TEST A HIDDEN SPOT.**
Begin with a wide nozzle (white/green), stand back 2–3 ft, test an out-of-sight area, move
closer only if needed. If you see etching, stripping, pitting, white marks, or wood fuzzing —
**back off immediately.**

### Nozzle color code (universal — memorize this)
- **Red (0°)** — pinpoint, most dangerous. **Never use on a customer's property.** Cuts skin,
  gouges everything. (Skip it entirely.)
- **Yellow (15°)** — concrete/flatwork only.
- **Green (25°)** — general all-arounder; concrete, patios, careful flatwork.
- **White (40°)** — siding, softer surfaces, rinsing.
- **Black (65°/soap)** — low-pressure soap/chemical application (this is your soft-wash tip).

### Soft wash vs pressure — the core mental model
- **Pressure washing** = high PSI does the work. **Only** for hard surfaces: concrete, brick
  (lower lows), pavers.
- **Soft washing** = low pressure (under ~500 PSI) + **chemistry** does the work. For
  **everything soft**: vinyl, painted wood, stucco, roofs. The bleach mix kills growth at the
  root so it stays gone 1–2 years; pressure alone just blasts the surface and it returns.
- **If Tommy points a pressure wand at vinyl siding, that is the rookie tell** and the way he
  cracks siding / forces water behind it / causes mold. House washing = soft wash, period.

### PSI by surface (the working ranges; sources vary, so START LOW within each)
| Surface | Working PSI | Method | What kills it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Concrete driveway** | 2,000–3,000 (surface cleaner) | pressure | Etching from a held wand; **tiger stripes** from no surface cleaner |
| **Brick** | 800–1,500, lower on old mortar | pressure (gentle) / soft | Blowing out **mortar joints**; older Shandon/Rosewood mortar is soft |
| **Pavers** | 1,000–2,500 | pressure | Blasting out **joint sand**, loosening stones |
| **Vinyl siding** | soft wash ~100–500 (chem does it) | **soft wash** | Cracking, water **behind** siding → mold/rot |
| **Wood deck/fence** | 500–1,200, with grain, 25–40° | **low pressure** | **Gouging/splintering**, lifting grain |
| **Stucco / EIFS** | very low / soft | **soft wash** | Cracking, chunking; needs patience |
| **Roof (asphalt/metal/tile)** | **NEVER pressure** | **soft wash / treatment only** | Stripping granules, voiding warranty, **falling off the roof** |
| **Windows / screens** | not with a washer | hand/low | Breaking glass, tearing screens |

### Chemistry Tommy will actually use (soft wash mix)
- **SH (sodium hypochlorite)** = "pool shock," the active ingredient. Sold at **10% or 12.5%**.
- **Surface-strength targets (rule of thumb):** **~0.5–1.5% on siding**, **higher (~3–4%) on
  concrete/masonry**. Duck runs **0.5–1% SH on siding**, **5–10 min dwell**.
- **Surfactant** ("soap" — Slo Mo, Hang Tite, or even Dawn in a pinch) makes the mix **cling**
  instead of running off plants and down walls. Non-negotiable on vertical/sloped surfaces.
- **Dwell time** = let it sit 5–10 min (longer in cold/shade) to kill growth, then rinse. The
  chemistry does the work; **don't crank pressure to compensate for skipping dwell.**
- **SH is perishable:** loses ~1–2% chlorine/week, faster in heat. Buy fresh, use within ~2–3
  weeks, store cool and dark.

### Universal safety / non-negotiables
- **Never spray off a ladder** (kickback knocks you off) and **never walk a wet roof.**
- **Never use the red 0° tip.** Water at these pressures **cuts skin.**
- **Always pre-wet and post-rinse all plants** near a soft-wash area — SH kills vegetation on
  contact. Cover sensitive plantings.
- **Keep water out of** electrical outlets, meters, AC units, gaps around windows/doors, under
  siding lap joints.
- **PPE:** eye protection, chemical-resistant gloves, closed-toe non-slip shoes, long pants.
  Never sandals.
- **Pre-1978 homes:** lead paint risk — don't blast painted surfaces on old homes without
  thinking; flag to customer.

## 4. Supplies / starter kit (on top of Bobby's borrowed washer)

Tommy has the machine (borrowed) and ~$1K. He does **not** need a $7,500 turn-key skid. He
needs a lean kit to do job one safely and look pro. Costs are realistic ranges (Home Depot /
local / online); confirm at purchase.

### THE ONE MUST-BUY: surface cleaner attachment
- A **15" surface cleaner** (universal ¼" quick-connect, fits most gas washers) is the single
  highest-leverage purchase. It's the spinning disc that gives an **even, streak-free concrete
  clean and prevents tiger stripes** — the #1 thing that separates a pro result from a streaky
  DIY wand job. **~$80–$150** at Home Depot (Westinghouse/DeWalt universal models).
- **Without it, Tommy will leave wand stripes on every driveway and lose the next job.**
  → **This goes on the Bucket D buy-before-job-one list.**

### The rest of the realistic starter kit
| Item | Why | Rough cost |
|---|---|---|
| **Nozzle tip set** (incl. white 40°, green 25°, yellow 15°, black soap) | Match nozzle to surface; lose the red 0° | **$10–$25** |
| **SH / pool shock (12.5%)** | The soft-wash active. Local pool-supply or hardware | **~$3–$6/gal**, a few gal to start |
| **Surfactant** (Slo Mo / Hang Tite; Dawn works to start) | Makes mix cling, saves chem, protects plants | **$20–$40** (or a bottle of Dawn) |
| **Pump/garden sprayer** (for applying mix without a fancy downstream rig) | Cheap soft-wash application to start | **$15–$40** |

| **Extra hose / 5-gal buckets** | Reach + mixing | **$20–$40** |

## 5. The curveball questions customers ask (anti-freeze bank → feeds Bucket C directly)

These are the questions that freeze Tommy live and cost the job. **16 real ones**, grouped.
Bucket C turns each into an "if-they-ask-X-say-Y" line. Short, honest, confident answers win —
Tommy doesn't need to be perfect, he needs to **not go blank.**

**Price / scope**
1. **"How much will it cost?"** — the big freeze. (Answer via the sq-ft/flat logic in §1; for
   wood/roof/complex: "Let me take a quick look so I quote it right, not guess.")
2. **"Why is your price different from the guy who quoted $X?"** — cheap bids skip protection
   (plant care, taping) and use harsh pressure that risks damage; price reflects doing it right.
3. **"Can you do my driveway and house at the same time?"** — yes, bundle discount; multi-
   surface is cheaper per surface than separate trips.
4. **"Do you charge by the hour or the job?"** — by the job/sq ft, quoted up front, no surprises.

**Time / logistics**
5. **"How long will it take?"** — most 1–2 story homes ~2–4 hrs incl. setup and cleanup;
   driveways ~1–2 hrs; depends on size/buildup.
6. **"Do I need to be home?"** — no, as long as there's water access and gate/pet access.
7. **"Do you use my water? Will it spike my water bill?"** — yes, garden-spigot water; usage is
   modest (similar to running a sink), pennies on the bill.
8. **"What if it rains / weather?"** — light rain is fine; if it's unsafe we reschedule, no charge.

**Damage / safety (the trust questions)**
9. **"Will this damage my siding / wood / windows?"** — no, because the right method is matched
   to the surface: soft wash (low pressure + cleaner) on siding/wood, pressure only on concrete.
10. **"Are you going to pressure wash my roof?"** — no — roofs get a low-pressure soft-wash
    treatment, never high pressure (it strips shingles). *(For now: "roofs aren't a service I
    offer yet" is the honest job-one answer.)*
11. **"Will the bleach kill my plants / hurt my pets?"** — plants are pre-wet and rinsed, mix is
    diluted and controlled; keep pets inside during service, fine once surfaces dry.
12. **"Are you insured?"** — *(Tommy's real gap — see Bucket D. Honest interim line matters; do
    not claim coverage that doesn't exist.)*

**Results / longevity**
13. **"How long will it stay clean / will the green stuff come back?"** — soft wash kills growth
    at the root, so it lasts ~1–2 years (vs plain water which just removes the surface and
    regrows fast); shade/trees shorten it.
14. **"Why does mold keep coming back?"** — water alone doesn't kill spores; needs the right
    solution + dwell. Also address moisture source (shade, gutters, drainage).
15. **"How often should I get this done?"** — Midlands humidity = every **1–2 years** for
    houses; annually for heavily shaded/tree-covered; driveways ~yearly.
16. **"Can you get this specific stain out?"** (oil, rust, red clay, paint) — honest answer:
    most come out; oil/rust/heavy clay need special treatment and may cost a bit more or need a
    second pass; some paint overspray may not be fully removable. **Don't over-promise.**

> **For Bucket C:** the highest-freeze items are **#1 (price)**, **#9 (damage)**, and **#12
> (insured)**. Build those answers first and tightest.

## 6. Common beginner mistakes that lose money or damage property

**Damage mistakes (the expensive ones):**
- **Wand-striping concrete** ("tiger stripes") by not using a surface cleaner — guarantees an
  ugly result and a callback. (Buy the surface cleaner.)
- **Pressure-washing siding** instead of soft-washing it — cracks vinyl, forces water behind it,
  strips paint.
- **Gouging/splintering wood** by too-high pressure or too-close/wrong nozzle on a deck or fence.
- **Etching concrete** by holding the wand too close in one spot (etched concrete is permanent).
- **Blowing out mortar or paver joint sand** with too much pressure on brick/pavers.
- **Roof damage / falls** — pressure on shingles strips granules; spraying from a ladder/roof is
  a fall risk. (Don't take roofs yet.)
- **Killing the customer's plants** by not pre-wetting/rinsing around an SH soft wash.
- **Using the red 0° tip** — damages surfaces and can cut skin.

**Money/relationship mistakes (the quiet killers):**
- **Under-quoting** by pricing a big multi-surface job like "a wash" — the #1 way Tommy works
  for $20/hr. (This is exactly why the §1 floor exists.)
- **Quoting wood/roof/complex jobs sight-unseen over the phone** and getting trapped at a number
  that doesn't cover the work.
- **Skipping dwell time** and cranking pressure to compensate — damages surfaces and still
  leaves growth that regrows fast → callback.
- **Over-promising stain removal** (oil, rust, old paint) and not delivering → unhappy customer,
  no referral.
- **No before/after photo** — loses the single best tool for selling the next job (the plan
  literally depends on this).
- **Skipping protection/prep** to save 10 minutes → property damage that eats the whole margin.
- **Buying expensive equipment before booking jobs** — money picks the upgrades, not excitement.
