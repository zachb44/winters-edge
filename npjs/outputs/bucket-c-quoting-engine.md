## 2. The quoting skeleton (the anti-freeze structure)

This is the fill-in-the-blank he runs every single time. Same five steps, same order,
every job. The whole point is to remove live thinking — he follows the steps instead of
inventing a process under pressure.

> **STEP 1 — What surface?**
> Driveway/concrete · House siding · Patio/walkway · Deck/fence/wood · *(Roof = "not yet")*
>
> **STEP 2 — Measure it (or pace it).**
> Concrete: length × width = sq ft. (Pacing: one big step ≈ 3 ft.)
> House: use the size buckets in §3 — don't measure every wall, match it to small/medium/large.
>
> **STEP 3 — Base rate × size = starting number.**
> Pull the rate from the §3 table for that surface. This is the *starting* number, not the final.
>
> **STEP 4 — Adjustments (what makes it bigger).**
> Two-story? Heavy growth/neglect? Oil/rust/clay stains? Hard access? Add per §3.
>
> **STEP 5 — Check the floor, then say the number.**
> Is the number above the §4 floor for the time it'll take? If yes, say it confidently.
> If no, re-scope or walk. **Never say a number that's under the floor.**

**The two rules that sit on top of the skeleton:**

- **Driveways and standard house washes = quote on the spot.** These have clean local rates.
- **Decks, fences, pool decks, anything wood, anything weird = "Let me take a quick look so I
  quote it right, not guess."** (Bucket A decision: no reliable local flat rate for wood; it's
  the surface most likely to get damaged or spark a re-stain conversation. In-person only.)

|---|---|---|

| **House siding (standard)** | **By size:** under 1,500 sf = $200–$300 · 1,500–2,500 sf = **$300–$450 (target ticket)** · 2,500–3,500 sf = $400–$550 · 3,500+ = $500–$600+ | Monster Pro + Shamrock agree on local sweet spot |

| **Patio / walkway** | **$0.20–$0.25/sq ft** — usually a **bundle add-on**, not a standalone trip | Duck (flatwork = concrete rate) |
| **Sidewalk add-on** | **$50–$100** tacked onto a driveway | Monster Pro |
| **Deck / fence / wood** | **QUOTE IN PERSON.** Start estimate $0.20–$0.40/sq ft, but always look first | Bucket A: no local flat rate, condition swings it |
| **Roof** | **Not a service yet.** ("I don't offer roofs yet.") | Bucket A: Phase 2, gear + fall risk |

### The incremental rule (Tommy's gut-calibrator)
> **Every extra 100 sq ft adds roughly $25–$50.** (Monster Pro.) Use this when a job is
> "a bit bigger than the last one" — don't re-quote from scratch, add per 100 sq ft.

### Adjustments — what moves a number UP (from Bucket A §2)

| Trigger | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| **Two stories** instead of one | **+30–50%** (more time, ladders, risk) |
| **Heavy growth / neglect** (5+ yrs, black mold, lichen) | **+20–40%** |
| **Oil stains** (driveway) | **+$25–$75** (degreaser) |
| **Rust stains** | **+$25–$50** (special remover) |
| **Deep red-clay staining** | extra passes — add time/cost, don't over-promise |
| **Porous surface** (brick/stucco vs vinyl) | **+$0.02/sq ft** (Duck) |
| **Hard access** (tight landscaping, slopes, lots to tape/bag) | add time — bill it |

### Bundle logic (raise the ticket honestly)
> Multi-surface is cheaper per surface than separate trips, so it wins the job AND raises
> the day's total. "Driveway + sidewalk together = $175–$400 with a multi-surface discount"
> (Monster Pro). **Always ask "want me to do the driveway/walkway while I'm here?"**

### The three job sizes (so Tommy sees the job correctly)

| Job | Realistic Midlands bill | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| **Small** | **$100–$250** | driveway only / small concrete. Competitive — bundle to raise it. |
| **Medium** | **$300–$450** | standard 1,500–2,500 sf house wash. **The target ticket.** |
| **Large** | **$500–$800+** | house + driveway + walk bundle, or 3,000+ sf home. A multi-surface day. |

> **The trap Bucket A named:** a $400 job and a $150 job can be "the same half-day of
> Tommy's life." He must **price the big one as the big one, not as 'a wash.'** That single
> habit is the difference between clearing his $60/hr target and slipping to $20/hr.

## 4. The hourly floor — the number that protects him

This is the most important part of the engine. Tommy wants to run cheap to win work. Fine —
**but cheap has a bottom**, and below that bottom he's paying the customer to work.

### Target vs. floor — say these two numbers right, because they are NOT the same

- **$60/hr is the TARGET on the meter** — the sticker rate he's aiming to quote at. It is
  **not take-home.** Gas, chemicals, and wear still come out of it.
- **~$50/hr NET is the FLOOR** — what's actually left *after* gas + chemicals + wear, divided
  by his real door-to-door hours. This is the real money.

The trap is reading $60 as "what I make." He doesn't make $60 — he quotes at $60 and *nets*
less after costs. The floor is set in NET terms on purpose, so the number that protects him is
measured in real money, not sticker price. $60 on the meter normally clears the $50 net
floor — but a big job he under-times can quote at $60 and still net under $50. That's why the
floor is checked in net, every time.

### What the meter rate ($60/hr) actually has to cover

Tommy's "$60/hr" is not $60 in his pocket. Out of it comes:

- **Chemicals** — SH + surfactant (real cost per job, more on heavy growth)
- **Gas** — both the truck/car to get there AND the washer running
- **Wear** — the machine (even borrowed, he's responsible), tips, hoses
- **His time** — including drive, setup, prep/taping, the actual wash, AND cleanup

So the real question is never "what's my hourly" — it's **"what does this job NET me per
hour of my life, door to door."**

### The floor math (simple, on purpose)

> **Step 1: Honest time estimate — door to door.**
> Not just spray time. Drive + setup + prep + wash + cleanup. Most home washes = **2–4 hrs**.
> Driveways = **1–2 hrs**. **When unsure, estimate HIGH** — under-estimating time is exactly
> how he ends up at $20/hr.
>
> **Step 2: Subtract job costs.**
> Chemicals + gas for that job. For a normal job, call it **~$20–$40** in materials/gas.
> (Heavy-growth jobs eat more chemical — bump it.)
>
> **Step 3: Net ÷ hours = real hourly.**
> (Quote − job costs) ÷ honest hours. **That** is what he actually made — the NET number the
> floor is measured against.

### THE FLOOR LINE (the walk-away rule)

> **If the job nets less than ~$50/hr of his actual door-to-door time, STOP. Re-scope or walk.**
>
> **~$50/hr NET is the floor. His $60/hr is the TARGET on the meter — a sticker rate, not
> take-home.** The target sits above the floor by design. The floor exists for the moment he's
> tempted to say a low number to win a job: **a job that nets under $50/hr is a job that's
> costing him, not paying him** — no matter what the meter rate looked like.

**Worked examples (using Bucket A local numbers):**

- **Standard house wash, 2,000 sf, light growth, one story.** Quote = **$375** (mid of
  $300–$450). Time, door to door = ~3.5 hrs. Costs ~$30. Net = $345 ÷ 3.5 = **~$98/hr net.**
  Well above floor. Say it confidently — this is a good job.
- **Two-car driveway, oil stains, easy access.** Base $200 + $50 oil = **$250.** Time ~2 hrs.
  Costs ~$25. Net = $225 ÷ 2 = **~$112/hr net.** Above floor. Good.
- **The trap job:** big 2,500 sf two-story house, heavy growth, lots of landscaping to protect
  — and Tommy's tempted to say **$300** to win it. Real time door-to-door = ~6 hrs. Costs ~$45.
  Net = $255 ÷ 6 = **~$42/hr net. BELOW FLOOR.** This is the exact job that pays him $20–$40/hr
  if he's not careful. Correct move: this is a **Large** job ($500–$800), two-story (+30–50%),
  heavy growth (+20–40%). Re-quote at **$550–$650**, or don't take it.

> **One-line version for the glance card:** *"Below $50/hr NET for your real time — door to
> door, after gas/chems/wear — means re-quote or walk. $60/hr is the target on the meter, not
> take-home. Not sure how long it'll take? You're about to under-quote."*

## 5. The if-they-ask-X-say-Y bank (the full anti-freeze tool)

All 16 curveball questions from Bucket A, each with a ready answer. **Tommy reads these — he
does not improvise.** The goal isn't to be perfect, it's to **not go blank.** Short, honest,
confident wins.

> **Where Tommy sees these:** the BIG 3 (#1 price, #9 damage, #12 insured) are on his live
> glance card, in their own boxes. **All 16** live on the at-home reference sheet
> (`tommy-facing/quoting-reference-sheet.md`) for him to get familiar with off the job. Bucket A
> flagged the big 3 as the highest-freeze items — they're built first and tightest below.

### The big three (on the glance card — memorize these cold)

**1. "How much will it cost?"** *(the big freeze)*
> "Depends on the size and surface — let me measure real quick and I'll give you a number
> right now." Then run the §2 skeleton. For wood/roof/complex: *"Let me take a quick look so
> I quote it right instead of guessing."*

**9. "Will this damage my siding / wood / windows?"**
> "No — because I match the method to the surface. Your siding gets a **soft wash** — low
> pressure and a cleaning solution that does the work, not high pressure. I only use real
> pressure on concrete. That's how you avoid damage."

**12. "Are you insured?"** *(Tommy's real gap — Bucket D, OPEN)*
> **Do NOT claim coverage that doesn't exist.** Honest interim line:
> *"I'm owner-operated and I'm careful — I protect your plants, fixtures, and windows before
> I start. I'm in the process of getting my general liability set up."* Then move the
> conversation to the work. **The real fix is filing the GL policy (Bucket D) — this answer
> is a bridge, not a destination.**

### Price / scope

**2. "Why is your price different from the guy who quoted $X?"**
> "Cheaper bids usually skip the protection — they don't pre-wet your plants or tape fixtures,
> and some use harsh pressure that can damage siding. My price is doing it right the first time."

**3. "Can you do my driveway and house at the same time?"**
> "Yeah — and it's cheaper to bundle than two separate trips. Want me to give you a combined number?"

**4. "Do you charge by the hour or the job?"**
> "By the job. I quote it up front so there are no surprises."

### Time / logistics

**5. "How long will it take?"**
> "Most homes are about 2–4 hours with setup and cleanup; driveways 1–2. Depends on size and buildup."

**6. "Do I need to be home?"**
> "No, as long as I've got water access and can get to the gate/pets."

**7. "Do you use my water? Will it spike my bill?"**
> "Yeah, I run off your outside spigot — usage is small, about like running a sink. Pennies on the bill."

**8. "What if it rains?"**
> "Light rain's fine. If it's unsafe we just reschedule, no charge."

### Damage / safety (the trust questions)

**10. "Are you going to pressure wash my roof?"**
> "No — roofs need a low-pressure soft-wash treatment, never high pressure; that strips the
> shingles. *(Job-one honest version: 'Roofs actually aren't a service I offer yet.')*"

**11. "Will the bleach kill my plants / hurt my pets?"**
> "I pre-wet and rinse all your plants, and the mix is diluted and controlled. Keep pets inside
> while I work — they're fine once everything dries."

### Results / longevity

**13. "How long will it stay clean / will the green come back?"**
> "A soft wash kills the growth at the root, so it lasts about 1–2 years. Plain water just
> rinses the surface and it comes back fast. Shade and trees shorten it."

**14. "Why does the mold keep coming back?"**
> "Water alone doesn't kill the spores — you need the right solution and time to sit. I also
> look at what's feeding it: shade, gutters, drainage."

**15. "How often should I get this done?"**
> "In this humidity, houses about every 1–2 years — yearly if you've got heavy shade or trees.
> Driveways about yearly."

**16. "Can you get this specific stain out?"** (oil, rust, red clay, paint)
> "Most come out. Oil, rust, and heavy clay need special treatment and might cost a little more
> or take a second pass. Some paint overspray may not fully come out — I'll be straight with you
> after I see it." **(Don't over-promise.)**

## 6. The "too big for one person" gut check

Tommy raised this fear directly: recognizing the job he can't do solo BEFORE he says yes.
Quick gut check — **if two or more are true, it's probably not a one-person job, and "let me
bring a hand" or "let me look first" beats a yes he regrets:**

- [ ] **Two-plus stories** with peaks he can't reach safely from the ground
- [ ] **Roof involved** (it's not a service yet — automatic "not now")
- [ ] **Big commercial-size flatwork** (parking lot, huge lot) — a full day-plus solo
- [ ] **Heavy growth on a large home** — the time doubles and the §4 floor math gets ugly fast
- [ ] **Anything needing a ladder + wand at the same time** (Bucket A: never spray off a ladder)
- [ ] **A timeline he can't hit alone** ("can you do all this by Saturday?")

> **The honest line when it's too big:** *"That's a bigger job than I want to rush solo — let
> me either bring a hand or take a real look and quote it right."* Brother-first reminder: it's
> not weakness to size a job honestly. Saying yes to a job that breaks him is the actual risk.

*(This gut check is condensed on the glance card and full on the reference sheet.)*
