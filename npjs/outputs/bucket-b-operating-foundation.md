## 1. Day-in-the-life structure

The point isn't a rigid schedule — Tommy won't follow a clock. The point is a SHAPE to
the day so he's never staring at "what do I do now." Three blocks, tied to the sun and
the day of the week, not to specific times.

### The shape

**MORNING = phone + paper.** Before any driving. This is when the business runs:
- Look at today's job cards (see §3 — they're already sitting on the truck visor).
- Return any missed calls/texts from yesterday. New lead → quote it (Bucket C skeleton).
- Confirm today's job: text the customer "on my way / see you at [time]."

**MIDDAY = job sites.** This is the part Tommy already knows how to do. Do the work,
take the before/after photo (the photo is part of the job, not optional — it's what
books the next one), collect payment before leaving.

**FRIDAY = wrap and quote only. Never START a job Friday.** This is Tommy's own rule
from the call, and it's a good one — keep it. Friday is: finish anything open, quote
next week's jobs, run the lead play, do the Sunday-prep so the check-in is painless.

### Why blocks, not a clock

A clock schedule is a maintenance system — it has to be looked at, updated, obeyed. A
SHAPE is just "mornings are for X, afternoons for Y, Fridays are different." Nothing to
maintain. The sun and the calendar push it; Tommy doesn't have to.

### The weekly lead play (lives inside the day shape, not separate)

Straight from the one-page plan, unchanged because it already passes — it's a specific
repeated ACTION, not "go find leads":
- Church Sunday → hand 5 cards to real people
- Text 3 past contacts
That's the whole top-of-funnel. It belongs to Friday/Sunday so it has a home.

## 2. Money movement, Tommy-scale

This is the hardest thing to strip, because Zach's real funnel has four moving parts
(business account → 20% tax pull → bill lump on the 1st → second lump on the 25th).
Tommy can't run four parts. He has almost no bills. So the goal here is **build the
habit while the money is small**, not manage complexity that doesn't exist yet.

### The one-number rule

Tommy tracks exactly ONE number day-to-day: **how much cash is in the business envelope
right now.** That's it. Not income, not expenses, not profit. One number, and it's
physical — he can see it by opening the envelope, not a balance he has to look up.

### The funnel, Tommy-scale (3 physical steps, done at the moment of payment — never "later")

When cash comes in from a job, before it goes anywhere, at the truck:

1. **Tax cut comes off the top, immediately.** A fixed share of every payment goes into
   a SECOND envelope marked **TAXES — NOT MINE**. This mirrors Zach's hidden tax account.
   The percentage is **Uncle Ron's call, not ours** — flagged in Bucket D. Until Ron
   sets it, use a conservative placeholder Tommy will recognize as "set aside, don't
   touch," and correct it once Ron weighs in. The principle Tommy needs to internalize:
   *the tax money was never his money.* It just passed through his hands.
2. **What's left goes in the main envelope.** That's the one number he tracks.
3. **Bills come out of the main envelope, not the tax envelope, ever.** Right now bills
   are near zero (he's at Dad's). So mostly the main envelope just grows — which is the
   point. It's down-payment money for the truck (one-page plan: walk onto the lot July 5
   with ~$3K down). The growing pile IS the proof the business works.

### Why envelopes and not a bank account / app

A business bank account is on the task list (Bucket D) and Tommy SHOULD get one — but
the funnel can't WAIT on it, and an app-based system fails the maintenance test on day
one. Physical envelopes are: already in view, zero maintenance, one-number-readable, and
impossible to "forget to check" because the cash is literally in his hand at payment
time. When the business account exists, the same two-envelope logic ports straight over
(main account + hidden tax sub-account, exactly like Zach's). The envelope is training
wheels for the funnel, not a permanent system.

### What Tommy does NOT do here

No tracking of expenses in a ledger. No categorizing. No monthly reconciliation. The
"books" for now are: receipts go in a third envelope (**RECEIPTS**) and get handed to
Uncle Ron at tax time. That's the entire bookkeeping system until there's enough volume
to justify more. Catching receipts in an envelope is not "doing paperwork" — it's
dropping paper in a box, which is the one filing motion Tommy already does naturally.

## 3. Job lifecycle — without a CRM, without maintaining anything

Lead → quote → scheduled → in progress → done → paid. Six stages. The trap is that every
CRM makes you UPDATE the stage. Tommy won't. So stage can't be a field he edits — it has
to be something he can SEE without doing anything.

### The mechanism: physical location = stage

One index card per job (name, address, phone, what they want, the quoted number). The
card's STAGE is wherever the card physically IS. He never "updates" a stage — he just
moves a piece of paper from one spot to the next as the job naturally moves, which is a
single physical motion, not an app action.

| Stage | Where the card lives | The trigger to move it |
|---|---|---|
| **Lead** (someone asked) | Left pocket of the truck visor caddy / clip | They called or texted |
| **Quoted** (gave a price) | Same caddy, behind a "QUOTED" divider | You told them a number |
| **Scheduled** (they said yes + a day) | Clipped to the day on the truck visor | They picked a day |
| **Done — not paid** | Flipped over on the dash | Work finished, no cash yet |
| **Paid — closed** | Dropped in the "DONE" envelope at home | Cash in hand |

The whole lifecycle is "move the card forward one spot when the obvious thing happens."
No screen. No typing. The stage is never wrong because the stage IS the location — there's
nothing to fall out of sync.

### Why an index card and not Jobber / an app

Tommy explicitly won't use a Jobber-type app right now — cost and overhead. He's right.
An app is a pull system (he has to open it) with maintenance (he has to update it). The
index card is a push system (it's clipped in front of his face on the visor) with zero
maintenance (moving paper isn't updating a record). When NPJS has enough volume that
paper cards become the bottleneck, revisit Jobber — not before. Foundation first.

### The one card that's always in the truck

Separate from job cards: ONE laminated card clipped to the visor permanently — the
quoting cheat-sheet + the lead play + the three Sunday questions. That's the "push"
surface. It doesn't move. It's the thing in his line of sight every time he gets in the
truck. (This is also the physical home for the Bucket C quoting skeleton once it's built.)

## 4. The "is this week good?" measure — the Sunday 3-number check-in

This is the entire accountability system. Do not add to it. It's already in the one-page
plan; Bucket B's job is just to formalize it and give it a home so it actually happens.

**Every Sunday, 10 minutes, Zach texts or calls Tommy. Three questions:**

1. **How many jobs did you book/complete this week?** (the motion number — first 30 days
   the target is 4 paid jobs total, any size; it's proof of motion, not revenue)
2. **What's the cash number right now?** (read straight off the main envelope — the one
   number)
3. **What's the one thing stuck?** (the blocker — Zach's job is to help clear it)

### Why this is push, not pull

Tommy doesn't run the check-in. ZACH runs it. Zach calling is the push mechanism — it's
the human version of Zach's transcript-auto-populate system. Tommy can't forget a check-in
that someone else initiates. All Tommy has to do is answer three things he already knows
off the top of his head (two of them are just "look at the envelope" and "count the
cards in the DONE envelope"). No prep, no spreadsheet, no report. If Tommy has to PREPARE
for the check-in, it failed — so the design is that the answers are already sitting in
physical view (cards + envelope) before Zach ever calls.

### The Sunday-prep that makes it painless (happens Friday, takes 2 minutes)

On Friday wrap: count the DONE-envelope cards (that's question 1), glance at the main
envelope (question 2). Question 3 he'll know without prep. That's it — and even this
"prep" is just reading two physical things he already has.

## 5. Tracking that survives ADHD — one surface, push not pull

Pulling it together: there is no spreadsheet, no app, no dashboard Tommy maintains. The
"system" is three physical surfaces, all already in his line of sight, none requiring an
update step:

1. **The truck visor** — today's job cards + the permanent laminated cheat-sheet card.
   This is the daily command center. He sees it every time he drives. Push: it's in his
   face. Maintenance: zero (cards move, nothing gets written/updated).
2. **Three envelopes** — MAIN (the one number), TAXES–NOT MINE (the hidden tax cut),
   RECEIPTS (hand to Uncle Ron at tax time). Plus a DONE envelope at home for closed-job
   cards. The money system and the records system, with no ledger.
3. **The Sunday call** — Zach-initiated, human push, three numbers. The accountability
   layer. Tommy maintains nothing; he answers.

### The single design principle behind all of it

Zach's whole approach is: *I'm bad at admin, so I automate it away and surface only the
one thing that matters, when it matters.* Tommy has no automation layer — so we replace
"automate" with "physical + human." The cards push themselves (they're on the visor).
The envelopes push themselves (the cash is in hand). The check-in pushes itself (Zach
calls). Nothing waits on Tommy to remember, open, or maintain. That's the translation of
ADHD guardrails into a no-tech context.

### When to add technology (not now)

The honest upgrade path, so nobody scope-creeps Tommy too early:
- **Business bank account** (Bucket D) → port the envelope funnel into it. Do this soon
  because cash-only has its own risks; but the two-envelope logic doesn't change.
- **Jobber or similar** → only when paper cards are an actual bottleneck (lots of jobs in
  flight at once). Not before.
- **Quote calculator tool** (parked, Phase 2) → built on Bucket C logic, removes live
  math, attacks the freeze. Good idea, not urgent.

Every one of these is "earn it with volume first." Foundation before features.
