## What this is

This is the back-end doc — the full reasoning for Zach and the system. Tommy never
reads this. Tommy reads the one-page version. This exists so the one-pager has a spine,
so a fresh chat can see WHY each choice was made, and so nothing gets re-litigated later.

The job was a translation, not research: take Zach's real operating systems (money
funnel, bill calendar, push-not-pull, ADHD guardrails) and strip them down to something
a box-in-the-closet brain runs with zero maintenance.

**The governing constraint, restated:** Tommy is more constrained than Zach — no meds,
no boss, lives at Dad's, almost no bills. Anything that requires "remember to," multiple
fields, or a multi-step update is dead on arrival. Single surface. Push not pull. No
maintenance.

## What changed in Rev 2 (and why)

Rev 1 passed the seed's five pass/fail tests, but those tests only check whether each
piece is *low-maintenance*. They don't check the harder ADHD question: **does the system
still work on the day Tommy forgets it exists?** Three places in Rev 1 quietly relied on
Tommy's in-the-moment discipline with no backstop. A box-in-the-closet brain will lose
those. Rev 2 fixes them:

1. **The tax split was happening at the truck, cash in hand, right after a job** — the
   single most ADHD-fragile moment in the day (hot, tired, distracted). The first "I'll
   split it later" silently breaks the tax envelope and nothing catches it. **Fix:** all
   job cash goes ONE place at the truck. The tax cut moves to ONE moment a week — on the
   Sunday call, with Zach on the phone. The split now inherits Zach's push instead of
   relying on Tommy's discipline at the worst possible moment.

2. **A lead that never became a card silently didn't exist.** Someone texts a job mid-task,
   Tommy doesn't make a card, and the system has no way to surface the miss — it only
   tracks what's already in it. **Fix:** the phone is the lead-catcher (texts already sit
   there, in view, unmissable). A card only gets created when a job reaches "quoted." No
   silent drop, and fewer cards to make.

3. **The Sunday call was push, but its ANSWERS had become pull** — Rev 1 gave Tommy a
   Friday prep step, contradicting its own rule that prep = failure. **Fix:** kill the
   prep. The two number-answers are glanceable in 10 seconds while Zach is already on the
   phone. Nothing to do in advance.

The rest of Rev 1 (the day shape, the visor as command center, the no-app stance) holds
and is unchanged.

## The five seed tests + the robustness test Rev 2 adds

The seed's five (still all must pass):

1. **Money system FAILS** if Tommy tracks more than ONE number day-to-day.
2. **Job lifecycle FAILS** if knowing a job's stage requires any update step Tommy
   initiates.
3. **Tracking surface FAILS** if using it requires opening anything not already in his
   physical line of sight.
4. **Anything FAILS** if a step starts with "remember to."
5. **Tommy-facing page FAILS** if he'd have to READ it top-to-bottom to use it.

The robustness test Rev 2 holds everything to (the "out of sight, out of mind" test):

6. **A process FAILS if its only safeguard is Tommy remembering to do it in the moment.**
   Every necessary process must either (a) happen automatically, (b) be impossible to skip
   because the trigger is physically in his hand/face, or (c) be carried by Zach's weekly
   push. If the honest answer to "what happens the week he forgets?" is "it silently
   breaks," it's not done.

See the **Self-test** at the bottom for how each piece scores against #6.

## 1. Day-in-the-life structure

The point isn't a rigid schedule — Tommy won't follow a clock. The point is a SHAPE to
the day so he's never staring at "what do I do now." Three blocks, tied to the sun and
the day of the week, not to specific times.

### The shape

**MORNING = phone + paper.** Before any driving. This is when the business runs:
- Look at today's job cards (see §3 — already sitting on the truck visor).
- Open texts/missed calls (the phone is the lead-catcher — see §3). New job that's real
  enough to price → make a card and quote it (Bucket C skeleton).
- Confirm today's job: text the customer "on my way / see you at [time]."

**MIDDAY = job sites.** The part Tommy already knows how to do. Do the work, take the
before/after photo (the photo is part of the job, not optional — it's what books the next
one), collect payment before leaving, and drop ALL the cash in the one truck envelope
(no splitting at the truck — see §2).

**FRIDAY = wrap and quote only. Never START a job Friday.** Tommy's own rule from the
call, and a good one — keep it. Friday is: finish anything open, quote next week's jobs,
run the lead play. (Note: Rev 1 had a "Sunday prep" task on Friday. Removed — see §4.
There is nothing to prep.)

### Why blocks, not a clock

A clock schedule is a maintenance system — it has to be looked at, updated, obeyed. A
SHAPE is just "mornings are for X, afternoons for Y, Fridays are different." Nothing to
maintain. The sun and the calendar push it; Tommy doesn't have to.

### The weekly lead play (lives inside the day shape, not separate)

Straight from the one-page plan, unchanged — it's a specific repeated ACTION, not "go
find leads":
- Church Sunday → hand 5 cards to real people
- Text 3 past contacts

It's anchored to church (a fixed weekly event already in Tommy's life) so it has a real
trigger, not a floating "remember to do outreach."

## 2. Money movement, Tommy-scale (REVISED)

This is the hardest thing to strip, because Zach's real funnel has four moving parts
(business account → 20% tax pull → bill lump on the 1st → second lump on the 25th).
Tommy can't run four parts. The goal is **build the habit while the money is small**, not
manage complexity that doesn't exist yet.

### The Rev 1 problem this fixes

Rev 1 asked Tommy to do a 3-envelope split at the truck the moment he got paid. That's
the most ADHD-hostile possible time to ask for an admin task — he just finished physical
work, he's hot, the customer's standing there, he wants to leave. The split is exactly
the kind of thing that gets skipped "just this once." And a skipped tax split is invisible:
nothing in the system shows that this week's tax money never got set aside. By the time
it matters (tax season), it's months of silent misses. **A safeguard that depends on
discipline at the worst moment of the day is not a safeguard.**

### The fix: one place at the truck, one split per week — on the call

**At the truck (every job, zero thinking):** ALL the cash goes into ONE envelope. That's
it. No math, no splitting, no decision. This is the only money move that happens in the
field, and it's a single motion he literally cannot get wrong.

**On the Sunday call (once a week, with Zach):** this is where the tax cut happens, and
it happens *while Zach is on the phone walking him through it.* The conversation already
asks "what's the cash number?" — so right after Tommy reads it, Zach says: "okay, move
[X] of that into the TAXES envelope now, while I'm here." The split is done, witnessed,
and impossible to silently skip — because if Tommy didn't do it, Zach knows in real time.

This is the core ADHD move: **the necessary-but-forgettable task (tax) gets welded to the
unforgettable event (Zach's weekly call).** Tommy never has to remember to set aside
taxes. The call remembers for him.

### The one-number rule (unchanged)

Day-to-day, Tommy tracks exactly ONE number: **cash in the MAIN envelope.** Physical,
openable, not a balance he looks up. The tax envelope isn't a number he tracks — it's a
weekly motion Zach prompts.

### The percentage

Still **Uncle Ron's call** — flagged in Bucket D as a hard dependency. Until Ron sets it,
use a conservative placeholder Tommy treats as "set aside, don't touch." Doing the split
weekly instead of per-job actually makes the % easier to correct later: it's one weekly
move to adjust, not a habit baked into every single transaction.

### Why a once-a-week split is fine here (not sloppy)

Per-transaction tax setting matters at volume. Tommy is at near-zero volume and near-zero
bills (lives at Dad's). The money mostly just accumulates as truck-down-payment proof.
Setting tax aside weekly vs. per-job changes nothing about what Ron eventually owes — the
total is the same — and it buys an enormous reliability gain: the split now actually
happens, every week, because Zach drives it. **A weekly split that happens beats a
per-job split that gets skipped.**

### Bookkeeping (unchanged): the RECEIPTS envelope

Receipts go in a third envelope and get handed to Uncle Ron at tax time. No ledger, no
categorizing, no reconciliation. Dropping a receipt in an envelope is the one filing
motion Tommy already does naturally (it's literally "throw the paper in the box," just
aimed at the right box). Backstop: if the receipts envelope is thin, Zach can eyeball that
on a visit — but it's low-stakes enough that "do your best" is acceptable here.

## 3. Job lifecycle (REVISED) — phone catches leads, cards track real jobs

Lead → quote → scheduled → in progress → done → paid. The trap: every CRM makes you UPDATE
the stage, and Tommy won't. So stage can't be a field he edits — it has to be something he
SEES without doing anything.

### The Rev 1 problem this fixes

Rev 1 made a card the moment "someone asked." Two failures: (1) it asks Tommy to do admin
(make a card) at the unpredictable moment a lead lands, often mid-job — exactly when he
won't; and (2) if he doesn't make the card, the lead vanishes with no trace. The system
couldn't tell him "you got a text Tuesday you never acted on."

### The fix: the phone is the lead inbox; cards start at "quoted"

**Leads live in the phone, not on a card.** When someone texts or calls, that message is
*already* sitting in his phone — in view, unmissable, zero effort to create because it
created itself. His texts/missed-calls list IS the lead list. The morning "phone + paper"
block is when he works it: anything in there he hasn't answered is an open lead, visibly
waiting. Nothing to make, nothing to lose. (Practical reinforcement: he can star/flag a
text he hasn't quoted, but even with zero flagging, an un-replied text is self-evidently
an open loop.)

**A card gets made only when he quotes a real job** — i.e. when it's worth tracking
through to paid. That's fewer cards (most leads never quote), made at a calmer moment
(he's deliberately quoting, not mid-task), for jobs real enough to deserve the effort.

### Physical location = stage (for carded jobs)

One index card per quoted job (name, address, phone, what they want, the quoted number).
The card's STAGE is wherever it physically IS. He never "updates" a stage — he moves paper
when the job naturally moves.

| Stage | Where the card lives | The trigger to move it |
|---|---|---|
| **Quoted** (gave a price) | Front of the visor caddy / clip | You made the card when you quoted |
| **Scheduled** (yes + a day) | Clipped to the day on the visor | They picked a day |
| **Done — not paid** | Flipped over on the dash | Work finished, no cash yet |
| **Paid — closed** | Dropped in the "DONE" envelope at home | Cash in hand |

(Leads sit in the phone, *before* this table. The card is born at Quoted.)

The whole lifecycle is "move the card forward one spot when the obvious thing happens."
No screen, no typing. The stage is never wrong because the stage IS the location.

### The backstop for a stuck card

Here's the ADHD-robustness piece: a card that sits too long in one spot is *visibly* stuck,
because it's physically in front of his face on the visor every time he drives. A "Done —
not paid" card flipped on the dash that's still there next week is an unmissable "you
didn't get paid yet" flag — no app reminder needed, the paper itself nags. And the Sunday
call catches the rest: "any card been sitting in the same spot all week?" is implicitly
question 3 (what's stuck).

### Why not Jobber / an app

An app is pull (he has to open it) + maintenance (he has to update it). The phone-as-inbox
+ index-card system is push (texts and cards are both already in his face) with near-zero
maintenance. Revisit Jobber only when paper cards become an actual bottleneck — not before.

## 4. The "is this week good?" measure (REVISED) — the Sunday call, zero prep

This is the entire accountability system AND the engine that drives the two things Tommy
won't do on his own (set aside tax, surface what's stuck). Do not add to it.

**Every Sunday, ~10 minutes, ZACH calls Tommy.** Four beats now — the original three
questions plus the tax move welded on:

1. **How many jobs did you book/complete this week?** (motion number — first 30 days, the
   target is 4 paid jobs total, any size; proof of motion, not revenue)
2. **What's the cash number right now?** (Tommy opens the MAIN envelope and reads it)
3. **→ Tax move, right here:** "Move [Ron's %] of that into the TAXES envelope now, while
   I'm on the phone." (Done, witnessed, can't be silently skipped.)
4. **What's the one thing stuck?** (the blocker — Zach helps clear it; also implicitly
   catches any card frozen in one spot all week)

### Why this is genuinely push, not pull (the Rev 1 fix)

Rev 1 said "if Tommy has to prepare for the check-in, it failed" — then handed him a
Friday prep step. Contradiction, removed. **There is no prep.** Everything the call needs,
Tommy produces live, in seconds, with things already in front of him:
- Q1: glance in the DONE envelope and count cards (or just recall — it's a handful).
- Q2: open the MAIN envelope, read the number.
- Q3 (tax): physically move cash between two envelopes that are right there.
- Q4: he knows what's stuck without looking anything up.

Zach initiating the call is the push. Tommy answering with zero preparation is the proof
it's push. If Tommy ever has to "get ready" for the Sunday call, something has crept back
in — pull it out.

### What makes this the keystone

This single weekly event carries three otherwise-forgettable processes: taxes (welded to
Q3), catching stuck jobs (Q4 + visible cards), and accountability (Q1). Tommy doesn't have
to remember any of them. He has to answer the phone on Sunday — and Zach makes sure that
happens. Everything load-bearing in the business hangs off one unforgettable event.

## 5. Tracking that survives ADHD — one surface, push not pull

There is no spreadsheet, no app, no dashboard Tommy maintains. The system is these things,
all either already in his line of sight or driven by Zach:

1. **The phone** — the lead inbox. Texts and missed calls create themselves and sit in
   view. Zero maintenance; it's the one device always in his hand.
2. **The truck visor** — today's job cards (born at Quoted) + the permanent laminated
   cheat-sheet card (quoting cheat-sheet + lead play + the Sunday beats). Seen every drive.
   A stuck card is a visible nag. Maintenance: moving paper, nothing written.
3. **The envelopes** — ONE truck envelope all field cash goes into; at home, MAIN (the one
   number), TAXES–NOT MINE (filled weekly on the call), RECEIPTS (hand to Ron), DONE (closed
   cards). No ledger.
4. **The Sunday call** — Zach-initiated. Carries tax, stuck-job detection, and the three
   numbers. The accountability engine.

### The single design principle

Zach's approach: *I'm bad at admin, so I automate it away and surface only the one thing
that matters, when it matters.* Tommy has no automation layer — so we replace "automate"
with "physical + Zach's weekly push." Leads push themselves (phone). Cards push themselves
(visor). The tax split and stuck-job sweep are pushed by Zach (call). Nothing load-bearing
waits on Tommy to *remember* — it waits on him to answer a text he can see or a phone call
Zach makes. That's ADHD guardrails translated into a no-tech, no-discipline-dependent
context.

### When to add technology (not now)

- **Business bank account** (Bucket D) → port the envelope funnel into it (main account +
  hidden tax sub-account). Do soon; cash-only carries its own risk. The weekly-tax-move
  habit ports over cleanly — it just becomes a transfer instead of moving paper cash.
- **Jobber or similar** → only when paper cards are an actual bottleneck. Not before.
- **Quote calculator** (parked, Phase 2) → built on Bucket C logic, removes live math,
  attacks the freeze. Good idea, not urgent.

Earn each one with volume first. Foundation before features.

## Self-test — re-run against ALL SIX tests, including the robustness test

- **#1 Money — one number day-to-day?** PASS. Day-to-day it's just MAIN-envelope cash.
  Field move is "all cash → one envelope" (no number, no split). Tax is a weekly motion,
  not a tracked number.
- **#2 Job stage — no self-initiated update?** PASS. Stage = card location. Leads don't
  even require a card (phone holds them). Advancing = moving paper when the real thing
  happens.
- **#3 Tracking surface — already in view?** PASS. Phone (in hand), visor (every drive),
  envelopes (cash in hand), Zach's call (comes to him). Nothing to open that isn't open.
- **#4 Zero "remember to"?** PASS. Re-scanned. Field cash is one motion; tax and
  stuck-jobs are driven by Zach's call; leads sit in the phone. No step relies on "remember
  to."
- **#5 Tommy-facing usable without reading?** Built to pass — see the one-pager. Scannable
  labels, answers in <5 seconds.
- **#6 Robustness — what breaks the week Tommy forgets?** PASS (this is the Rev 2 point):
  - *Taxes:* welded to Zach's call. If Tommy forgets, Zach prompts. Can't silently break.
  - *Leads:* live in the phone, which created them and keeps them in view. A forgotten lead
    is a visibly un-replied text, not a vanished one.
  - *Job stage:* a stuck card is physically in his face on the visor + caught on the call.
  - *Accountability:* Zach initiates; Tommy can't forget a call someone else makes.
  - The one remaining Tommy-discipline dependency is *dropping field cash into the one
    truck envelope* — but that's a single frictionless motion with cash literally in hand,
    the lowest-failure-rate point in the whole system, and the Sunday cash-count would
    expose a chronic problem fast.

### The honest residual risk (name it, don't hide it)

The whole structure leans on **Zach actually making the Sunday call, every week.** That's
now the single point of failure — by design, because it's better to concentrate the
dependency on the reliable person (Zach) than spread it across the unreliable moments
(Tommy's post-job exhaustion). But it means: if Zach goes quiet for three weeks, taxes
stop getting set aside and stuck jobs stop getting caught. **Mitigation:** the Sunday call
should live in Zach's own push system (the thing that surfaces his deadlines), not Tommy's.
Put it where Zach can't miss it. Flagged to Bucket D.
