## What this is

This is the back-end doc — the full reasoning for Zach and the system. Tommy never
reads this. Tommy reads the one-page version. This exists so the one-pager has a spine,
so a fresh chat can see WHY each choice was made, and so nothing gets re-litigated later.

The job was a translation, not research: take Zach's real operating systems (money
funnel, bill calendar, push-not-pull, ADHD guardrails) and strip them down to something
a box-in-the-closet brain runs with zero maintenance.

**The governing constraint, restated:** Tommy is more constrained than Zach — no meds,
no boss, lives at Dad's, almost no bills. Anything that requires "remember to," multiple
fields, or a multi-step update is dead on arrival. Single surface. Push not pull. No
maintenance.

## What changed in Rev 3 (and why)

Rev 2 made the system ADHD-robust by welding the tax set-aside and the stuck-job sweep to
a weekly Sunday call between Zach and Tommy. That fixed the "what breaks the week Tommy
forgets" problem — but it created a new one: it turned a warm, brother-to-brother phone
call into a business-critical operational hub. The day the call slips, taxes silently stop
and stuck jobs stop getting caught. And loading "must happen or the business breaks" onto a
relational call makes it *less* likely to happen, not more — it becomes a chore.

**Rev 3 removes that single point of failure by tying the money mechanic to the money
itself, not to a conversation:**

1. **The tax split comes off the Sunday call and welds to the cash instead.** The truck
   envelope becomes a one-way box; the only way to take money out is to empty the whole
   thing, and emptying it triggers the split. The trigger is *Tommy wanting his money* — a
   want he already has, on his own floating schedule — not a call Zach has to make. See §2.

2. **The stuck-job sweep comes off the call's critical path.** It rides on the physical
   card surface Tommy already looks at every drive — a stuck card is visibly stuck whether
   or not the brothers talk. The call, if it happens, is a friendly second catch, not the
   mechanism. See §3.

3. **The Sunday call goes back to what the one-page plan always said it was:** a relational,
   10-minute, brother-to-brother check-in. Three light questions. Nothing operational rides
   on it. Nothing financial breaks if a given Sunday gets missed. See §4.

The Rev 2 instincts that were right are preserved: ALL field cash goes into ONE envelope at
the truck (one can't-fail motion, no splitting in the field), leads live in the phone, and
there's zero call prep. Rev 3 only changes *what the tax split is anchored to.*

The rest (the day shape, the visor as command center, the no-app stance) holds and is
unchanged.

## The five seed tests + the robustness test

The seed's five (still all must pass):

1. **Money system FAILS** if Tommy tracks more than ONE number day-to-day.
2. **Job lifecycle FAILS** if knowing a job's stage requires any update step Tommy
   initiates.
3. **Tracking surface FAILS** if using it requires opening anything not already in his
   physical line of sight.
4. **Anything FAILS** if a step starts with "remember to."
5. **Tommy-facing page FAILS** if he'd have to READ it top-to-bottom to use it.

The robustness test (the "out of sight, out of mind" test):

6. **A process FAILS if its only safeguard is Tommy remembering to do it in the moment.**
   Every necessary process must either (a) happen automatically, (b) be impossible to skip
   because the trigger is physically in his hand/face, or (c) ride on something he already
   wants to do anyway. If the honest answer to "what happens the week he forgets?" is "it
   silently breaks," it's not done.

   **Rev 3 sharpens #6:** "carried by Zach's weekly push" is no longer an acceptable answer
   for anything *financial.* A weekly human call is not a guaranteed event. The money
   mechanic must survive a missed call. Only the relational check-in is allowed to depend on
   the call — because if the check-in is missed, nothing breaks.

See the **Self-test** at the bottom for how each piece scores against #6.

## 1. Day-in-the-life structure

The point isn't a rigid schedule — Tommy won't follow a clock. The point is a SHAPE to
the day so he's never staring at "what do I do now." Three blocks, tied to the sun and
the day of the week, not to specific times.

### The shape

**MORNING = phone + paper.** Before any driving. This is when the business runs:
- Look at today's job cards (see §3 — already sitting on the truck visor).
- Open texts/missed calls (the phone is the lead-catcher — see §3). New job that's real
  enough to price → make a card and quote it (Bucket C skeleton).
- Confirm today's job: text the customer "on my way / see you at [time]."

**MIDDAY = job sites.** The part Tommy already knows how to do. Do the work, take the
before/after photo (the photo is part of the job, not optional — it's what books the next
one), collect payment before leaving, and drop ALL the cash in the one truck envelope
(no splitting at the truck — see §2).

**FRIDAY = wrap and quote only. Never START a job Friday.** Tommy's own rule from the
call, and a good one — keep it. Friday is: finish anything open, quote next week's jobs,
run the lead play. (Note: Rev 1 had a "Sunday prep" task on Friday. Removed — there is
nothing to prep.)

### Why blocks, not a clock

A clock schedule is a maintenance system — it has to be looked at, updated, obeyed. A
SHAPE is just "mornings are for X, afternoons for Y, Fridays are different." Nothing to
maintain. The sun and the calendar push it; Tommy doesn't have to.

### The weekly lead play (lives inside the day shape, not separate)

Straight from the one-page plan, unchanged — it's a specific repeated ACTION, not "go
find leads":
- Church Sunday → hand 5 cards to real people
- Text 3 past contacts

It's anchored to church (a fixed weekly event already in Tommy's life) so it has a real
trigger, not a floating "remember to do outreach."

## 2. Money movement, Tommy-scale (Rev 3 — decoupled from the call)

This is the hardest thing to strip, because Zach's real funnel has four moving parts
(business account → 20% tax pull → bill lump on the 1st → second lump on the 25th).
Tommy can't run four parts. The goal is **build the habit while the money is small**, not
manage complexity that doesn't exist yet.

### What Rev 3 fixes

Rev 1 split the cash three ways *at the truck* (worst ADHD moment — hot, tired, customer
watching). Rev 2 fixed that by moving the split to the Sunday call with Zach prompting it
live — but that welded a load-bearing financial task to a phone call that might not happen.
A skipped call meant a skipped tax set-aside, silently, until tax season.

**Rev 3 ties the split to the money itself.** The trigger is no longer a call or a
calendar — it's Tommy's own want for his cash. He won't go long without reaching for his
money, and the moment he reaches for it, the split fires. His appetite for the cash *is*
the recurring trigger. Nothing financial depends on the brothers talking.

### The fix: the truck envelope is a one-way box; touching it triggers the split

**At the truck (every job, zero thinking):** ALL the cash goes into ONE envelope. No math,
no splitting, no decision. A single motion he cannot get wrong. *(This part of Rev 2 was
right — keep it.)*

**The truck envelope is a ONE-WAY box. Money goes in at jobs. It does not come out a twenty
at a time.** The only way to take money out is to empty the WHOLE thing — and emptying it is
what triggers the split. He can't cherry-pick spending cash out of the truck; to touch it at
all, he dumps it all.

**The split, welded to his own want for the money:** any time he needs cash out of the truck
envelope (payday feeling, a bill, wants to spend it), the rule is one fixed motion:

> **1. Empty the WHOLE truck envelope onto the table.**
> **2. Tax comes off the top FIRST — 20% → the TAXES–NOT MINE envelope.**
>    *(Use 20% now. It's the working number — deliberately a little high, so if anything
>    Tommy gets money back at tax time, never comes up short. Uncle Ron may set the real %
>    later (Bucket D), but 20% is what he uses today. A conservative number he can act on
>    beats a "correct" instruction with no number — without a number, step 2 stalls.)*
> **3. What's left is MAIN. THAT is your money. Spend from MAIN, never from the truck.**

**The order is the safeguard, and it is non-negotiable: empty → tax off the top → THEN it's
yours.** If he pulls spending money out first and means to "split the rest later," the tax
cut is dead — "later" never comes. So the tax never gets to sit in his hand feeling like
his. It comes off before the spendable number exists. Same principle as a tax sub-account
hidden from your own view: the money is gone before it feels like yours to touch.

### The pinch valve (so the one-way rule survives the first real Tuesday)

The one-way rule only works if it's absolute — the first time Tommy grabs a twenty out of
the truck "just this once," the rule is broken for good. But a pinch *will* happen: he'll be
somewhere needing $20 and the only cash is in the truck envelope. The fix isn't to loosen
the rule — it's to make breaking it self-correct:

> **If you ever have to pull from the truck envelope in a pinch — empty the whole thing and
> split it right then. Don't take the twenty and leave the rest. Touching it = emptying it,
> every time, no exceptions.**

This keeps the rule absolute while acknowledging the pinch is inevitable. The pinch becomes
just another trigger for the same empty-split-spend motion, instead of the crack that kills
it.

### Why this can't drift

There's no rhythm to remember and nothing to schedule — but it also can't go too long,
because the cap is *Tommy's own appetite for the cash.* He won't go weeks without wanting
his money, and the moment he reaches for it, the split fires. Push-native: the system runs
on a want he already has, not a discipline he has to summon, and not a call Zach has to
remember to make.

### The one-number rule (unchanged)

Day-to-day, Tommy tracks exactly ONE number: **cash in the MAIN envelope.** Physical,
openable, not a balance he looks up. The truck envelope isn't a number he tracks (it's a
one-way holding box). The TAXES envelope isn't a number he tracks (it's "not mine, don't
touch"). MAIN is the one number — and it's already the honest spendable number, because tax
came off before anything landed in MAIN.

### The percentage

The real % is still **Uncle Ron's call** — flagged in Bucket D as a hard dependency. But
"wait for Ron" cannot mean "no number to use this week," or the motion stalls. So Tommy uses
**20% now** as the working set-aside (Zach's own number), treated as "set aside, don't
touch." If Ron later sets a different number, it's a one-word change to the rule — the motion
doesn't change, just the percentage in step 2.

### Why a per-withdrawal split is fine here (not sloppy)

Per-transaction tax setting matters at volume. Tommy is at near-zero volume and near-zero
bills (lives at Dad's). The money mostly just accumulates as truck-down-payment proof.
Splitting per-withdrawal vs. per-job changes nothing about what Ron eventually owes — the
total is the same — and it buys the reliability gain that matters: the split actually
happens, because it rides on Tommy reaching for his own money instead of on a call.

### Bookkeeping (unchanged): the RECEIPTS envelope

Receipts go in a third envelope and get handed to Uncle Ron at tax time. No ledger, no
categorizing, no reconciliation. Dropping a receipt in an envelope is the one filing
motion Tommy already does naturally (it's literally "throw the paper in the box," just
aimed at the right box). Backstop: if the receipts envelope is thin, Zach can eyeball that
on a visit — but it's low-stakes enough that "do your best" is acceptable here.

## 3. Job lifecycle (Rev 2, unchanged) — phone catches leads, cards track real jobs

Lead → quote → scheduled → in progress → done → paid. The trap: every CRM makes you UPDATE
the stage, and Tommy won't. So stage can't be a field he edits — it has to be something he
SEES without doing anything.

### The fix: the phone is the lead inbox; cards start at "quoted"

**Leads live in the phone, not on a card.** When someone texts or calls, that message is
*already* sitting in his phone — in view, unmissable, zero effort to create because it
created itself. His texts/missed-calls list IS the lead list. The morning "phone + paper"
block is when he works it: anything in there he hasn't answered is an open lead, visibly
waiting. Nothing to make, nothing to lose. (Practical reinforcement: he can star/flag a
text he hasn't quoted, but even with zero flagging, an un-replied text is self-evidently
an open loop.)

**A card gets made only when he quotes a real job** — i.e. when it's worth tracking
through to paid. That's fewer cards (most leads never quote), made at a calmer moment
(he's deliberately quoting, not mid-task), for jobs real enough to deserve the effort.

### Physical location = stage (for carded jobs)

One index card per quoted job (name, address, phone, what they want, the quoted number).
The card's STAGE is wherever it physically IS. He never "updates" a stage — he moves paper
when the job naturally moves.

| Stage | Where the card lives | The trigger to move it |
|---|---|---|
| **Quoted** (gave a price) | Front of the visor caddy / clip | You made the card when you quoted |
| **Scheduled** (yes + a day) | Clipped to the day on the visor | They picked a day |
| **Done — not paid** | Flipped over on the dash | Work finished, no cash yet |
| **Paid — closed** | Dropped in the "DONE" envelope at home | Cash in hand |

(Leads sit in the phone, *before* this table. The card is born at Quoted.)

The whole lifecycle is "move the card forward one spot when the obvious thing happens."
No screen, no typing. The stage is never wrong because the stage IS the location.

### The stuck-job sweep rides on the cards, not the call (Rev 3)

A card that sits too long in one spot is *visibly* stuck, because it's physically in front
of his face on the visor every time he drives. A "Done — not paid" card flipped on the dash
that's still there next week is an unmissable "you didn't get paid yet" flag — no app
reminder, no call needed, the paper itself nags every single drive.

**This is now the primary stuck-job mechanism, and it does not depend on the Sunday call.**
The card surface catches it whether or not the brothers talk. If the call happens, "anything
been sitting all week?" is a friendly second catch — but the card was already nagging him on
its own. Rev 2 leaned on the call for this; Rev 3 moves it onto the surface that's in his
face anyway.

### Why not Jobber / an app

An app is pull (he has to open it) + maintenance (he has to update it). The phone-as-inbox
+ index-card system is push (texts and cards are both already in his face) with near-zero
maintenance. Revisit Jobber only when paper cards become an actual bottleneck — not before.

## 4. The Sunday call — just a call to his brother (Rev 3)

Rev 2 made this call the operational hub of the whole business — taxes, stuck-job sweep, and
accountability all hung off it. Rev 3 takes all of that off. **The Sunday call is now exactly
what the one-page plan always described: a warm, ~10-minute, brother-to-brother check-in.
Nothing operational rides on it. It is relational, not a system component.**

Why the change: the moment a relational call becomes "must happen or the business breaks,"
it stops being warm and becomes a chore — and given how both brothers are wired, a chore
gets skipped. Worse, when it got skipped under Rev 2, real financial damage happened silently
(no tax set aside). Rev 3 removes the stakes from the call so it can just be a call — and
moves the load-bearing stuff (tax, stuck jobs) onto surfaces that don't depend on it (§2 the
money, §3 the cards).

### The three light questions (from the one-page plan)

**Every Sunday-ish, ~10 minutes, Zach calls Tommy. Three questions, no prep, nothing to do:**

1. **How many jobs did you book/complete this week?** (motion number — first 30 days, the
   target is 4 paid jobs total, any size; proof of motion, not revenue)
2. **What's the cash number right now?** (Tommy opens the MAIN envelope and reads it — and
   because tax already came off the top at every withdrawal, MAIN is already the honest
   number; nothing to move on the call)
3. **What's the one thing stuck?** (the blocker — Zach helps clear it. The cards already
   surface stuck *jobs* on the visor; this question is just the friendly human version)

### What the call no longer does

- **No tax move on the call.** Tax is handled at the money — off the top, every time Tommy
  empties the truck envelope (§2). The call never touches it.
- **No stuck-job dependency.** The cards nag on the visor on their own (§3). The call is a
  second catch, not the mechanism.
- **No prep, ever.** Everything the call needs, Tommy produces live in seconds from things
  already in front of him. If he ever has to "get ready" for it, something crept back in.

### What happens if a Sunday gets missed

**Nothing breaks.** Tax still got set aside (it happens at withdrawal, not on the call).
Stuck jobs still nag from the visor. The only thing lost is a friendly catch-up — which they
pick back up the next week. That is the entire point of Rev 3: the call is allowed to be
imperfect because nothing load-bearing hangs on it.

## 5. Tracking that survives ADHD — one surface, push not pull

There is no spreadsheet, no app, no dashboard Tommy maintains. The system is these things,
all either already in his line of sight or riding on something he already wants to do:

1. **The phone** — the lead inbox. Texts and missed calls create themselves and sit in
   view. Zero maintenance; it's the one device always in his hand.
2. **The truck visor** — today's job cards (born at Quoted) + the permanent laminated
   cheat-sheet card (quoting cheat-sheet + lead play + the three Sunday questions). Seen
   every drive. A stuck card is a visible nag. Maintenance: moving paper, nothing written.
3. **The envelopes** — ONE truck envelope (one-way box, all field cash goes in); at home,
   MAIN (the one number, already tax-clean), TAXES–NOT MINE (filled off the top at every
   withdrawal), RECEIPTS (hand to Ron), DONE (closed cards). No ledger.
4. **The Sunday call** — Zach-initiated, relational only. Three light questions. Carries no
   financial or operational load.

### The single design principle

Zach's approach: *I'm bad at admin, so I automate it away and surface only the one thing
that matters, when it matters.* Tommy has no automation layer — so we replace "automate"
with "physical + ride on what he already wants." Leads push themselves (phone). Cards push
themselves (visor). The tax split rides on Tommy reaching for his own cash (the one-way box).
Stuck jobs nag from the visor. Nothing load-bearing waits on Tommy to *remember* — or on
Zach to *call.* That's ADHD guardrails translated into a no-tech, no-discipline-dependent,
no-single-point-of-failure context.

### When to add technology (not now)

- **Business bank account** (Bucket D) → port the envelope funnel into it (main account +
  hidden tax sub-account). Do soon; cash-only carries its own risk. The one-way-box habit
  ports over cleanly — the tax sub-account becomes the "off the top" destination, and a real
  hidden sub-account is even better at "gone before it feels like yours" than an envelope is.
- **Jobber or similar** → only when paper cards are an actual bottleneck. Not before.
- **Quote calculator** (parked, Phase 2) → built on Bucket C logic, removes live math,
  attacks the freeze. Good idea, not urgent.

Earn each one with volume first. Foundation before features.

## Self-test — re-run against ALL SIX tests, including the robustness test

- **#1 Money — one number day-to-day?** PASS. Day-to-day it's just MAIN-envelope cash, and
  MAIN is already tax-clean (tax came off at withdrawal). Truck envelope is a one-way box (no
  number), TAXES is "not mine" (no number).
- **#2 Job stage — no self-initiated update?** PASS. Stage = card location. Leads don't
  even require a card (phone holds them). Advancing = moving paper when the real thing
  happens.
- **#3 Tracking surface — already in view?** PASS. Phone (in hand), visor (every drive),
  envelopes (cash in hand). Nothing to open that isn't open.
- **#4 Zero "remember to"?** PASS. Re-scanned. Field cash is one motion; tax rides on Tommy
  wanting his money; stuck jobs nag from the visor; leads sit in the phone. No step relies on
  "remember to" — and none relies on "remember to call," either.
- **#5 Tommy-facing usable without reading?** Built to pass — see the one-pager. Scannable
  labels, answers in <5 seconds.
- **#6 Robustness — what breaks the week Tommy forgets (or the week the call doesn't
  happen)?** PASS (this is the Rev 3 point):
  - *Taxes:* welded to the money. Tax comes off the top every time Tommy empties the truck
    envelope to get his cash — a thing he does because he wants the money. Survives a missed
    call entirely. The pinch valve keeps the one-way rule absolute.
  - *Leads:* live in the phone, which created them and keeps them in view. A forgotten lead
    is a visibly un-replied text, not a vanished one.
  - *Job stage:* a stuck card is physically in his face on the visor. No call required.
  - *Accountability / relational:* the Sunday call — and it's the ONLY thing that depends on
    the call. If it's missed, nothing breaks; they catch up next week.

### The honest residual risk (name it, don't hide it)

Rev 2's single point of failure was "Zach must make the call every week or taxes stop." Rev 3
**removes that** — the money no longer depends on the call. The residual risk is now smaller
and sits in one place: **Tommy following the empty → tax-off-top → spend order every time he
takes money out**, including in a pinch. That's a single motion riding on a want he already
has (his own cash), with the pinch valve closing the obvious loophole. It's the lowest-failure
point available, because it's powered by Tommy's own appetite for the money rather than by
memory or by someone else's phone call. Backstop: the Sunday cash-count would expose a chronic
problem (e.g. a TAXES envelope that's stayed empty) reasonably fast — as a catch, not as the
mechanism.
