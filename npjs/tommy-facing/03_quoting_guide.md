## The whole thing in five steps

Every quote, no matter the job, runs the same five steps in the same order. When somebody asks how much, you're not thinking "uh, what do I charge?" — you're just running the steps.

> **Step 1 — What surface is it?**
> Driveway/concrete? House siding? Patio or walkway? (Wood or roof? You don't quote those on the spot — more on that below.)
>
> **Step 2 — How big is it?**
> Concrete: pace it off. Length times width = square feet. (One big step is about 3 feet.) House: don't measure every wall — just match it to a size bracket (small, medium, large) in the price list below.
>
> **Step 3 — Base price.**
> Look up the rate for that surface and size in the list below. That's your starting number.
>
> **Step 4 — Does anything make it bigger?**
> Two stories? Heavy mold and growth? Oil or rust stains? Hard to get around? Each of those adds to the number. (Amounts below.)
>
> **Step 5 — Check it against the floor, then say it.**
> Make sure the number isn't below your floor for the time it'll take (the floor is explained below). If it clears, say it out loud, confident, no flinching.

That's the whole skeleton. Five steps. The rest of this doc is just filling in the numbers for steps 3, 4, and 5.

## The prices (these are real Columbia numbers — they're yours)

These come from actual power washing companies working in your market. This is what your neighbors charge and what customers around here actually pay. Trust them.

### Driveways / concrete
| Size | Price |
|---|---|
| One-car | $100–$175 |
| Two-car | $150–$250 |
| Three-car | $225–$300 |
| Big / circular | $275–$350 |

Or by the foot: **$0.20–$0.25 per square foot.**

### Houses (your bread-and-butter job)
| Size | Price |
|---|---|
| Under 1,500 sq ft | $200–$300 |
| **1,500–2,500 sq ft** | **$300–$450 ← your target** |
| 2,500–3,500 sq ft | $400–$550 |
| 3,500+ sq ft | $500–$600+ |

### Patios / walkways / sidewalks
About **$0.20–$0.25/sq ft** — but the real move is **bundling** these onto a driveway or house. A sidewalk added to a driveway is an easy extra **$50–$100**. Always ask: *"Want me to hit the walkway while I'm here?"* It raises the ticket and barely adds time.

### The two you do NOT quote on the spot
- **Wood (decks, fences):** the price depends too much on the condition, and wood is the easiest thing to damage. Say: *"Let me take a quick look so I quote it right instead of guessing."*
- **Roofs:** not a service you offer yet. Say: *"I don't do roofs yet."* Clean and honest.

### The handy rule for "a little bigger than last time"
Every extra 100 square feet adds roughly **$25–$50.** So when a job's just a bit bigger than one you've done, don't re-figure the whole thing — add per 100 feet and move on.

## What makes a number go up (Step 4)

The same driveway can be a $150 job or a $250 job depending on what you're walking into. Here's what bumps it, and by how much:

| What you see | What it adds |
|---|---|
| **Two stories** instead of one | **+30–50%** (more time, ladders, risk) |
| **Heavy growth / neglect** (black mold, lichen, years of buildup) | **+20–40%** |
| **Oil stains** on a driveway | **+$25–$75** |
| **Rust stains** | **+$25–$50** |
| **Hard access** (tight landscaping, slopes, lots to protect) | add time — bill it |

You don't need to memorize these. You need to *look* at the job and notice the things that make it harder, because every one of them is time, and time is money you're either charging for or giving away.

## The floor — the number that protects you (Step 5)

This is the most important part of the whole doc, so slow down here.

You're going to be tempted to quote cheap to win jobs. That's fine — being competitive is smart. **But cheap has a bottom, and below that bottom you're paying the customer for the privilege of working.** The floor is the line that keeps you from doing that to yourself.

### Your two numbers — and they are NOT the same

- **$60 an hour is your target** — the rate you're aiming to quote at. But that's *not* what ends up in your pocket. Gas, chemicals, and wear on the machine all come out of it.
- **$50 an hour NET is your floor** — "net" means *after* those costs come out, divided by the *real* hours the job takes you, door to door. This is the actual money.

The mistake is reading "$60/hour" as "I make $60 an hour." You don't. You quote at $60, and after your costs you keep less. The floor is set in *net* terms on purpose, so the number protecting you is measured in real money, not the sticker price.

### How to check the floor (simple math)

> **1. Be honest about the time — door to door.** Not just spray time. Drive there, setup, prep, the wash, cleanup, drive back. Most house washes are 2–4 hours. Driveways 1–2. **When you're not sure, guess HIGH** — underestimating time is exactly how you end up working for $20 an hour.
>
> **2. Subtract your costs.** Gas plus chemicals for that job — usually $20–$40.
>
> **3. What's left, divided by the hours, is what you actually made.** If that number's under $50 an hour, the job's too cheap. Re-quote it higher or walk away.

### See it in real numbers

- **House wash, $375, takes about 3.5 hours, $30 in costs.** That's $345 ÷ 3.5 = **about $98/hour.** Way above the floor. Say it with confidence — good job.
- **Two-car driveway with oil, $250, about 2 hours, $25 in costs.** That's $225 ÷ 2 = **about $112/hour.** Above the floor. Take it.
- **The trap:** a big two-story house, heavy growth, lots of landscaping — and you're tempted to say **$300** to win it. But it's really a 6-hour day. $255 ÷ 6 = **about $42/hour. That's below the floor.** This is the exact job that pays you $20–$40 an hour if you're not careful. The right move: it's a big two-story with heavy growth, so it's a **$550–$650 job** — quote that, or let it go.

That last one is the whole reason the floor exists. A big ugly job and a small easy job can eat the same chunk of your day. **Price the big one as the big one.** If you only remember one thing from this entire doc, remember that.

## The three questions that freeze you (and what to say)

When somebody throws a question you weren't ready for, you don't have to be perfect — you just have to **not go blank.** Here are the three that catch everybody, with an answer ready to go. Read them out loud a few times so they're in your mouth, not just your head.

**1. "How much will it cost?"**
> *"Depends on the size and surface — let me measure real quick and I'll give you a number right now."* Then run your five steps. (For wood or anything complicated: *"Let me take a quick look so I quote it right instead of guessing."*)

**2. "Will this damage my siding / wood / windows?"**
> *"No — I match the method to the surface. Your siding gets a soft wash: low pressure and a cleaning solution that does the work, not high pressure. I only use real pressure on concrete. That's how you avoid damage."*

**3. "Are you insured?"**
> *"I'm owner-operated and careful — I protect your plants, fixtures, and windows before I start, and I'm getting my general liability set up now."* (Don't claim a policy you don't have yet. This is the honest line until that's filed — and a customer asking it is your nudge to get it done.)

There are more questions customers ask — about how long it takes, whether they need to be home, whether it'll come back — and you'll learn those answers fast just by doing jobs. These three are the ones that freeze people, so these are the three to have ready cold.

## One gut check before you say yes to a big one

Before you agree to a job, ask yourself if it's too big to do alone. If two or more of these are true, it probably is:

- Two or more stories with spots you can't reach safely from the ground
- A roof is involved (not yet — automatic no)
- A huge commercial-size lot — a full day-plus by yourself
- Heavy growth on a big house — the time doubles fast
- You'd need a ladder and the wand at the same time (never spray off a ladder)
- A deadline you can't hit alone ("can you do all this by Saturday?")

If it's too big, the honest line is: *"That's a bigger job than I want to rush solo — let me bring a hand or take a real look and quote it right."* Sizing a job up honestly isn't weakness. Saying yes to a job that breaks you is the actual risk.

## The whole doc in five lines

1. **Run the five steps every time:** surface → size → base price → what makes it bigger → check the floor → say it.
2. **The prices are real and they're yours.** Houses at $300–$450 are your target job.
3. **Bundle the small stuff** (walkways, sidewalks) onto the big stuff. Always ask.
4. **The floor is $50/hour NET — after costs, on your real time.** $60 is the target, not take-home. Guess time HIGH.
5. **Price the big job as a big job.** That one habit is the difference between $60/hour and $20/hour.

That's quoting. Run the steps, trust the numbers, protect your floor, and say your price like you mean it. The calculator's coming to make this even easier — but once you've done a dozen jobs with this guide, you won't need it. You'll just know.
