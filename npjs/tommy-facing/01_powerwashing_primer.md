## 2. What power washing actually is

Most people think power washing is "point the wand, pull the trigger, blast the dirt off." That's the part that loses people money and wrecks property. The real trade is understanding that **different surfaces need completely different approaches**, and knowing which is which is the whole game.

There are really two jobs hiding inside "power washing," and you need to know the difference cold.

### Pressure washing — high pressure does the work

This is what people picture: high-pressure water cutting through grime. It works on **hard surfaces only** — concrete driveways, sidewalks, patios, brick (gently). The pressure itself is what cleans.

### Soft washing — chemistry does the work, not pressure

This is the part nobody outside the trade knows, and it's the part that makes you a pro instead of a guy with a machine. For **anything soft** — vinyl siding, painted wood, stucco — you do **not** blast it with high pressure. You use **low pressure and a cleaning solution**. The chemical kills the dirt, mold, and algae; the water just rinses it. The mix does the work.

Here's why this matters more than almost anything else in this doc: **if you point a high-pressure wand at someone's vinyl siding, you will damage their house.** You'll crack the siding, or worse, you'll force water *behind* it, and now there's mold growing inside their wall that they won't find for months. That's the rookie mistake that ends businesses. Direct high-pressure spray is the leading cause of siding damage — going over roughly 1,000 PSI drives water behind the panels, cracks the vinyl, and can void the manufacturer's warranty.

So the mental model, burn it in:

> **Hard surface (concrete) = pressure does the work.**
> **Soft surface (siding, wood) = chemistry does the work, low pressure just rinses.**

There's a bonus to soft washing that's also your sales pitch: because the chemical kills the growth at the *root*, it stays clean for one to two years. A guy who just blasts it with water is only rinsing the surface, and the green comes back in months. You do it right, it lasts. That's worth money and you should say so.

## 3. How the work actually works

Let's walk through the two surfaces you'll do most — a house and a driveway — start to finish, so the actual job isn't a mystery.

### Washing a house (soft wash)

1. **Walk it first.** Before any water, walk the whole house. Note the plants, the outlets, the lights, the AC unit, anything you need to protect or avoid. Look at which sides have the most growth — the shady, north-facing walls are usually the worst.
2. **Protect the plants.** This is not optional. The cleaning mix (it's basically a controlled bleach solution) will kill or burn plants if you let it. So you **soak every plant and bush near the house with plain water first.** Fully hydrated plant cells resist the chemical — a plant that's already full of clean water can't soak up much of the mix. Wet them before, rinse them after.
3. **Mix and apply the solution — bottom to top.** You apply the cleaning mix starting at the *bottom* of the wall and work *up*. This sounds backwards but it matters: working from the bottom up prevents streaking. If you start at the top, the mix runs down over dry siding and leaves clean streaks you can't get rid of.
4. **Let it sit (dwell time).** Give the chemical 5 to 10 minutes to actually work. This is the part beginners skip — they want to start rinsing immediately. Don't. The chemistry needs time to kill the growth. Skipping the dwell and cranking up pressure to compensate is exactly how you damage siding *and* leave growth that grows right back.
5. **Rinse — top to bottom.** Now you rinse with clean, low-pressure water, working from the *top* down, so the dirty water runs down over siding you haven't rinsed yet.
6. **Rinse the plants again.** Hit all the landscaping with clean water one more time. Done.

That's a house. Two to four hours for most homes including setup and cleanup.

### Washing a driveway (pressure wash)

1. **Clear and sweep.** Move cars, planters, anything in the way. Sweep off loose dirt and leaves — if you leave them, they turn to mud and get pushed into a slurry that's a pain to clean up.
2. **Pre-treat the stains.** Oil spots, rust, heavy grime — hit them with the right cleaner first and let it sit 10–15 minutes to break down. This gives the detergent time to break down the grime so the pressure washer can lift it away more easily.
3. **Use the surface cleaner attachment — not the wand.** This is the single most important thing for a good driveway. The surface cleaner is the round, flat, spinning attachment. It spreads the pressure evenly and gives you a clean, streak-free result. If you clean a driveway with just the wand, you get **"tiger stripes"** — ugly uneven lines all over the concrete — and a callback. Those stripes are caused by inconsistent technique; the best way to prevent them is to use a surface cleaner attachment.
4. **Slow, overlapping passes.** Walk slow. Overlap each pass a few inches. Don't speed up, don't stop in one spot. Move slowly and at a steady pace to avoid cleaning one area too much, which leaves rings.
5. **Rinse it down.** Rinse the loosened gunk away with a wide nozzle, working from the high end of the driveway toward the street so the dirty water runs off.

A pro trick worth knowing: if you do get some light striping, a **post-treatment** — spraying the cleaning mix back over the finished concrete and letting it dry without rinsing — evens it out and keeps it cleaner longer. Apply another coat of concrete cleaner and let it sit to remove these stripes.

Driveways run one to two hours.

## 4. The nozzles — your color code

Your washer comes with a set of colored nozzle tips. Each color is a different spray angle. Using the wrong one is how people wreck stuff, so learn these. Lower number = narrower, more dangerous spray.

| Color | Angle | Use it for |
|---|---|---|
| **Red** | 0° | **Never. Leave it in the box.** It's a pinpoint jet that cuts skin and gouges every surface. There is no job where you need it. |
| **Yellow** | 15° | Concrete and flatwork only. Stubborn stains on a driveway. |
| **Green** | 25° | The general all-arounder — concrete, patios, careful flatwork. |
| **White** | 40° | Siding, softer surfaces, rinsing. The gentle one. |
| **Black** | 65° / soap | Low-pressure soap tip — this is what puts your cleaning solution *on* the surface. Your soft-wash applicator. |

The one to never forget: **the red 0° tip is a property-destroyer and a skin-cutter. You will never use it.** Everything else, match the color to the surface — wide and gentle (white) for soft stuff, narrower (yellow/green) for concrete.

## 5. What people pay for this around Columbia

Here's the part that makes it real. These aren't made-up numbers — they come from three actual power washing companies operating in your exact market (Columbia, Lexington, Irmo) plus real reported job prices from Richland and Lexington counties. This is what your neighbors are actually charging and what customers are actually paying.

### Driveways / concrete
- One-car driveway: **$100–$175**
- Two-car: **$150–$250**
- Three-car: **$225–$300**
- Big or circular: **$275–$350**
- Or by the foot: about **$0.20–$0.25 per square foot**

Driveways are the most competitive, most price-shopped service — lots of solo guys out there doing them. Don't expect to get rich on a driveway alone. It's a foot in the door and a thing to **bundle** onto a bigger job.

### Houses (this is your bread and butter)
- Under 1,500 sq ft: **$200–$300**
- **1,500–2,500 sq ft: $300–$450 ← this is your target job**
- 2,500–3,500 sq ft: **$400–$550**
- 3,500+ sq ft: **$500–$600+**

The standard house wash in the $300–$450 range is the job you want to get good at landing. Two of the three local companies agree that's the sweet spot for a normal home.

### Patios, walkways, sidewalks
Priced like concrete — about **$0.20–$0.25/sq ft** — but the real play is **bundling** them onto a driveway or house wash. A sidewalk added to a driveway is an easy extra **$50–$100**. Always ask: "want me to hit the walkway while I'm here?"

### What you don't quote over the phone yet
- **Decks, fences, wood** — there's no clean local price for these because the condition changes everything. Wood is also the easiest surface for a beginner to damage. Always look in person before you give a number.
- **Roofs** — not a service you offer yet. Roofs need special soft-wash gear you don't have and involve real fall risk. The honest answer is "I don't do roofs yet." That's fine.

### How a $150 job becomes a $400 job

This is the thing to actually understand, because it's how you stop underpricing yourself. The same house can be a cheap job or an expensive one depending on:

- **Size** — the big driver. Bigger = more.
- **One story vs. two** — two-story adds 30–50% because of the time, the ladders, the risk.
- **How bad the growth is** — light dirt is quick; heavy black mold and algae is slow and eats chemical. Heavy neglect adds 20–40%.
- **Stains** — oil on a driveway adds $25–75, rust adds $25–50, they take special treatment.
- **Access** — tight landscaping, slopes, lots of stuff to protect and tape off = more time = more money.

The trap that catches every beginner: a small easy job and a big ugly job can take **the same half-day of your life**, but if you price the big one like "just a wash," you worked all day for nothing. We'll build the system to catch that in the quoting doc. For now, just know: **see the variables, price the big one as the big one.**

## 6. The gear you need (and why)

You've got the washer from Bobby and about a thousand bucks. Good news: you do **not** need a $7,000 professional rig to start. You need a lean kit to do a safe, pro-looking job. Here's the list and, more important, *why* each thing matters.

### The one thing you can't skip: a surface cleaner
A **15-inch surface cleaner attachment** — the spinning disc thing — is the single most important purchase, around **$80–$150**. This is what gives you that even, streak-free driveway. Without it, every driveway you do has tiger stripes, and tiger stripes lose you the next job. **Buy this before job one.** It's the difference between looking like a pro and looking like a guy who rented a machine.

### The rest of the starter kit
- **Nozzle tip set** (~$10–$25) — the colored tips from Section 4, so you can match the spray to the surface.
- **Sodium hypochlorite / "pool shock"** (~$3–6/gal) — this is the active ingredient in your soft-wash mix. It's what kills the mold and algae. You'll buy the 12.5% strength and dilute it down.
- **Surfactant / "soap"** ($20–40, or a bottle of Dawn to start) — this makes your mix *cling* to a wall instead of running straight off. Without it the chemical slides off before it can work. Not optional on siding.
- **A pump sprayer or garden sprayer** ($15–40) — a cheap way to apply your soft-wash mix when you're starting out.
- **Safety gear** ($30–50) — eye protection, chemical-resistant gloves, closed-toe non-slip shoes. Never do this in sandals. The chemicals are no joke and the surfaces get slick.
- **A bucket and measuring jug** ($10–20) — for mixing your solution to the right strength.

**Realistic total: roughly $200–$375** on top of the borrowed washer. That leaves you cushion inside your budget. The surface cleaner is the one thing not to cheap out on; everything else can start basic and upgrade as the money comes in.

### A word on the chemistry, so it's not scary
Your soft-wash mix is mostly three things: **pool shock (the killer), water (the dilution), and a little soap (makes it stick).** On siding you want a fairly weak mix — the pros run roughly half a percent to one and a half percent strength on a wall. On concrete you can go stronger. The shock loses strength over a couple weeks, especially in heat, so **buy it fresh and use it up** — don't stockpile gallons that'll be dead by next month.

## 7. A day in the life

So what does actually doing this look like? Here's a normal day, roughly.

**Morning is the office.** Before you drive anywhere, you handle the business from your phone and a few cards. Check your texts and missed calls — those are your leads, sitting right there. Answer the real ones. Confirm today's job: text the customer "on my way." This is when the business runs; the rest of the day is labor.

**Midday is the work.** You drive to the job, and the first thing you do is walk it and protect what needs protecting. Then you do the wash — house or driveway, the process from Section 3. **Take a before-and-after photo. Every time. It's part of the job, not an extra** — that photo is what books your next customer. When you're done, you get paid before you leave, and the cash goes straight into your money system (that's the next doc).

**The work is physical.** You're on your feet, outside, in South Carolina heat and humidity, dragging hoses and equipment around. It's hard on your back and knees. You're on your feet, handling equipment, and working outdoors in various weather conditions. That's the honest truth of it. It's good, satisfying work — you can see exactly what you accomplished — but it's work.

**Fridays are for wrapping up, not starting.** Finish anything open, quote next week's jobs, and don't start a brand-new big job on a Friday. That's a good rule you already came up with. Keep it.

## 8. How customers actually find you

You don't need to spend money on advertising to start. Solo guys in this trade get their first customers the same handful of ways, and they all cost basically nothing:

- **Word of mouth and referrals.** This is the big one early. You do a good job, the customer tells a neighbor, the neighbor calls you. One real network connection can hand you a string of jobs.
- **Before-and-after photos.** The single best marketing you have. A driveway that's half-clean, half-filthy in one picture sells itself. Take them on every job and you'll always have something to show.
- **Facebook and Nextdoor.** Post your work in local groups and on Nextdoor. People constantly ask for recommendations on these; being the guy with photos of real local jobs is how you get picked.
- **A free Google Business Profile.** Costs nothing, and it's how you show up when someone searches "pressure washing near me" in Columbia. Worth setting up once you've got a few jobs and photos under your belt.

You don't have to do all of these at once. Early on it's mostly **referrals + photos + handing out cards to real people.** The fancier stuff comes later. The work and the photos do most of the selling.

## 9. The beginner mistakes that cost money

Quick honest list of how guys lose money or wreck a job doing this. None of these are hard to avoid once you know them.

**The ones that damage property:**
- **Pressure-washing siding** instead of soft-washing it. Cracks the vinyl, drives water behind it. The #1 expensive mistake.
- **Tiger-striping a driveway** by using the wand instead of a surface cleaner.
- **Gouging wood** with too much pressure on a deck or fence.
- **Killing the plants** by not pre-wetting and rinsing them.
- **Using the red 0° tip.** On anything. Ever.

**The ones that quietly cost you money:**
- **Underpricing the big job** by treating it like a small one. The single biggest way to work hard for nothing.
- **Quoting wood or complex jobs sight-unseen** over the phone and getting trapped at a number that doesn't cover the work.
- **Skipping the dwell time** and cranking pressure to make up for it — damages the surface and the growth comes back anyway.
- **Over-promising on stains.** Some oil, rust, and old paint won't fully come out. Be straight about it up front instead of failing to deliver.
- **Forgetting the before-and-after photo** — you just threw away your best tool for getting the next job.
- **Buying expensive gear before you've booked jobs.** Let the money decide your upgrades, not your excitement.
