## Before you read this

The first doc was about the *work* — what power washing is and how to do it. This one's about the *business* — everything around the work that turns "a guy who washes stuff" into an actual business that makes money on purpose.

Here's the good news up front: a business, any business, really only has three parts. Once you see those three parts, the whole thing stops being overwhelming and starts being a checklist. That's what this doc does — it shows you the three parts, explains what each one means, and gives you a dead-simple way to handle each.

I'm going to teach you a little bit of the language too, because it's worth knowing. Don't let the words scare you — they're just labels for stuff you already understand.

Let's go.

---

## The whole business in one picture: the funnel

Picture a funnel — wide at the top, narrow at the bottom. That's your business, and everything that happens in it falls into one of three stages:

- **Top of the funnel:** getting people to know you exist. **Leads.**
- **Middle of the funnel:** turning those people into booked, agreed-on jobs. **Winning the work.**
- **Bottom of the funnel:** doing the job and getting paid. **Delivering and collecting.**

That's it. That's the entire business. Every single thing you do all day lands in one of those three buckets. People in business call this "the funnel" because a lot of people come in the wide top (you talk to ten people), fewer make it to the middle (four want a quote), and fewer still come out the bottom (two actually book and pay). That's normal. That's how it works for everybody.

So let's take them one at a time.

---

## TOP OF FUNNEL — Leads, leads, leads

A **lead** is just a person who might pay you. That's all it means. Somebody who texted you, somebody who asked at church, somebody who saw your work on a driveway and wants their own done. Every one of those is a lead.

Top of the funnel is the one job you can never stop doing: **making sure there's always a next person.** The day you stop putting out leads is the day, two weeks later, you have no work. It's the part most people let slide when they get busy, and then they hit a dead week and panic. Don't let it go quiet.

### What you're already doing (and it works)

You've already got the most powerful lead source there is: **people who know you, and people who've seen your work.** Referrals and word of mouth are how guys in this trade actually get started — you do a good job, the customer tells a neighbor, the neighbor calls. One happy customer can turn into three jobs. Keep doing that. It's not "beneath" a real business — it *is* the business early on.

### Other ways to get leads (pick what's easy, ignore the rest)

You don't need all of these. You need one or two you'll actually do. Here they are, easiest first:

- **Before-and-after photos.** Take them on every job — I said this in the first doc and I'll say it again here because it's also your best marketing. A half-clean, half-filthy driveway in one photo sells the next job better than anything you could say.
- **Hand cards to real people.** Physical business cards, handed to actual humans — at church, neighbors, anybody. Old-fashioned and it still works.
- **Post in local Facebook groups and Nextdoor.** People are constantly asking "anybody know a good pressure washing guy?" in these. Be the guy who shows up with real photos of real local jobs. Costs nothing.
- **A free Google Business Profile.** This is how you show up when somebody Googles "pressure washing near me" in Columbia. Set it up once you've got a few jobs and photos. Free.

Don't try to do all four this week. Start with the photos and the cards — the stuff you're already near — and add the others when you've got room.

### The simplest possible way to keep track of leads

Here's the thing nobody tells you: **your leads are already organized, for free, and you don't have to do anything.**

Every text and every missed call is sitting right there in your phone. That *is* your lead list. You don't need an app, a spreadsheet, or a system. When somebody reaches out, it's in your phone. The only "tracking" you do is: in the morning, look at your texts and missed calls, and answer the ones you haven't answered yet. An unanswered text is an open lead. That's the whole system.

Why am I making a point of "the simplest possible way"? Because you and I both have the kind of brain that won't keep up a complicated tracking system. If it takes ten steps and a reminder, we won't do it, and we both know it. So the rule for everything in this business is: **if it's not already in front of your face, it doesn't get done.** Your phone is always in your hand. So your phone is the lead list. Done.

## MIDDLE OF FUNNEL — Getting the job across the finish line

Okay, somebody reached out. Now what? The middle of the funnel is everything between "a person is interested" and "we have a deal." For you, that's basically two things: **quoting the job, and locking in the day.**

### Quoting is where the money is won or lost

When somebody says "how much?" — that's the moment. That's the single most important skill in this whole business, and it's the thing that freezes people. It freezes everybody at first. That's why it gets its own doc (the third one). For now, just understand *where it sits*: quoting is the middle of the funnel. It's the gate between a lead and a paid job, and getting good at it is most of the game.

The one principle to carry from here into the quoting doc: **a confident, fair number, said out loud without flinching, wins the job.** A number you mumble or second-guess loses it. The third doc gives you a system so you're never guessing — that's what makes the confidence real instead of fake.

### Locking in the day

Once they say yes to a number, you book a day. "Great — I can do Thursday morning, that work?" Pick a day, say it, write it down where you'll see it. The job isn't real until there's a day attached to it.

### The thing that quietly wins middle-of-funnel: just answering

Here's a stupid-simple edge most people throw away: **answer people.** A huge number of jobs are lost simply because the washer never texted back, or took three days to do it. If you answer your leads the same day — even just "hey, saw your message, can I swing by tomorrow to take a look?" — you'll beat half your competition without trying. Slow and flaky loses jobs. Quick and straight wins them.

## BOTTOM OF FUNNEL — You won the job. Now what?

This is the part that feels like the whole job but is actually just one-third of the business. You won the work — now you deliver it and you collect. Three things live down here: **doing the work, getting paid, and handling the money once you've got it.**

### Doing the work

This is the part from the first doc — the actual washing. Walk the property, protect what needs protecting, do the job right, and **take the before-and-after photo** (which, notice, just fed the *top* of your funnel again — that photo is your next lead). Do good work and the bottom of the funnel quietly refills the top. That's how a business gets a flywheel going.

### Getting paid — before you leave

Collect payment **before you drive away.** Not "I'll send an invoice," not "catch you next week" — money in hand before the truck moves. This is the single most common way new guys get burned: great job, friendly customer, "I'll Venmo you tonight," and then it's a week of awkward texts chasing your own money. Get paid at the job. Every time.

### Handling the money once you've got it

Here's where I want to teach you something that'll save your ass at tax time, and I'm going to keep it stupid simple because complicated money systems are exactly the kind of thing our brain abandons.

The problem with cash coming in is that **it all feels like yours, and it isn't.** Some of it is the government's (taxes), and if you spend it all as it comes in, tax season is a disaster. So we separate it — but in the simplest way that exists.

**Here's the whole system. Three envelopes.**

- **One envelope rides with you** (call it the work envelope). Every dollar you collect on a job goes straight in. No sorting, no math, no thinking at the job — just put it in. One motion you can't get wrong while you're tired and the customer's standing there.
- **When you want to actually use your money, you empty that whole envelope out at home and split it:**
  - **First, taxes come off the top.** Take 20% out and put it in a second envelope marked something like "NOT MINE — TAXES." Do this *first*, before you touch any of it for spending. (20% is the number to use for now — Uncle Ron may adjust it later, but use 20% today so you're never caught short.)
  - **What's left is actually yours.** That goes in your third envelope (or your pocket, or the bank) and that's your real money to spend.
- **Receipts** — anything you buy for the business, throw the receipt in a fourth envelope. Hand it to Uncle Ron at tax time. No sorting, no logging. Just don't throw them away.

Why the "take taxes off the top first" order matters: if you spend first and plan to "set aside the tax part later," later never comes. We both know it doesn't. So the tax comes out *before* the money ever feels like yours to spend. Once it's in the NOT-MINE envelope, pretend it doesn't exist.

That's the entire money system. One envelope at the job, split it when you want your money, taxes off the top first, receipts in a pile for Ron. You're not running books. You're just keeping the government's slice separate from yours so nothing blows up in April.

> **One real note:** cash is fine to start, but a business bank account is a good near-term step — Uncle Ron can set you straight on that, and it makes the "separate the tax money" part automatic instead of an envelope. That's a conversation for him, not something to figure out yourself.

## A note on staying on track (for brains like ours)

I want to be straight with you about something, brother to brother, because we share this wiring: **the hardest part of this won't be the washing. It'll be the remembering.** Following up with that lead, setting the tax money aside, not letting a finished-but-unpaid job slip your mind. That's the part our kind of brain drops, not because we're lazy but because if it's out of sight, it's gone.

So I'm not going to hand you a planner and tell you to be disciplined. That doesn't work for us and I'm not going to pretend it does. Instead, the whole system above is built so the important stuff is **physically in front of you** instead of living in your memory:

- Leads live in your phone, which is already in your hand.
- The tax split happens when you reach for your own money — a thing you'll do anyway.
- Receipts go in an envelope, not a spreadsheet.

**Here's one idea that helps a lot of people with our wiring, take it or leave it:** the windshield visor in whatever you're driving is a great "command center," because you look at it every time you get in. Some guys keep a couple index cards clipped there — one for each job they've quoted — and the card's *position* tells them where the job stands (still need to follow up, scheduled, done-but-not-paid). When a finished job is still sitting up there unpaid at the end of the week, it's staring at you every drive. It's a way of making the easy-to-forget stuff impossible to ignore.

That's just one idea. The point isn't the index cards specifically — it's the principle: **for stuff we tend to forget, put it somewhere our eyes already go.** However you end up doing that is fine. Find what works for you.

1. **A business is three parts: get leads (top), win the job (middle), do it and get paid (bottom).** That's the whole funnel.
